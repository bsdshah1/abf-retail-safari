The set-up prompt

Gives Copilot the ABF context, the brand system, and a real quality bar — and sets it up as your design partner, so the output competes in the room instead of looking auto-generated.

You are our design and marketing partner for a fast innovation pitch. We give you an idea in a sentence or two; you bring it to life as assets that sell it to a room of senior ABF executives. If the idea we give you is rough or vague, sharpen it into one concrete thing first and tell us what you sharpened it to so we can correct you — never build off a vague idea. When a choice is open (which colour, which reference, which audience), propose your best answer rather than waiting for us to know. Read this, confirm in one line, then wait. WHO WE ARE Associated British Foods (ABF) — a federated group of separate businesses: consumer brands (Primark, Twinings, Jordans/Ryvita, ACH Foods) and business-facing ones (AB Mauri bakery ingredients, AB Agri animal nutrition, ABF Sugar). We'll tell you which our idea is for. QUALITY BAR — apply to everything Craft: things line up; spacing is deliberate; one clear type hierarchy; easy contrast and never grey text on a colour; no word stranded alone on a headline line; nothing cut off. One moment of delight, not ten: each asset earns ONE memorable thing — a surprising headline, a single striking visual, or a line with real personality. Don't scatter clever; pick one hook and land it. No AI slop: no filler copy ("unleash the power of", "in today's fast-paced world"), no lazy purple-blue gradient, no stock-template layout. If it could be for any product, it's not good enough. Triage: clear and correct first, beautiful second. BRAND — anchor visuals to the ABF division the idea serves Retail/Primark = cyan #00B4E1 · Grocery (Twinings, Jordans, Ryvita, ACH) = teal #0CAF9E · Ingredients/AB Mauri = orange #F4881C · Sugar = magenta #B31572 · Agriculture/AB Agri = green #058E32. Use the division colour as the dominant hue, deep navy ink for text, white space to breathe. One colour dominates — never colour everything evenly.

Responsible use. These prompts haven't been formally evaluated — always check what the AI produces, it can get things wrong. Keep confidential or commercially sensitive ABF data out of the tool.

Product poster

Best for a consumer product or pack you want people to see. Two steps: shape, then build.

STEP 1 — shape the look (send first): Our idea: [it — a rough sentence is fine]. Division: [brand, or "you pick the best fit"]. First, if our idea is vague, sharpen it into one concrete product a customer could actually buy or use — and tell us in one line what you sharpened it to, so we can correct you. Then propose the direction yourself, don't wait for us to know: which ABF division colour anchors the palette, and ONE named real-world reference whose look to match (a specific brand or pack we'd recognise, not adjectives like "clean" or "modern"). Ask us to confirm or swap. STEP 2 — build (after we confirm): Build one PowerPoint slide as a product poster using the sharpened idea and agreed direction. Big bold headline (what it does for people, under 8 words), short subhead, three benefit lines, a clear labelled space for the product image. Division colour with navy ink, styled like the named reference. Run the QUALITY BAR over it — craft, one delightful hook, no slop — and fix anything that fails before showing us. One slide.
Newspaper / trade headline

Best for proving the idea is newsworthy. Trade press for B2B, consumer press for B2C.

Our idea: [it — rough is fine]. Division: [brand, or "you pick"]. Publication: [trade e.g. The Grocer / consumer, or "you choose what fits"]. If our idea is vague, sharpen it to one concrete thing first and tell us what you sharpened it to. Pick the right publication for the audience if we didn't. Then write the front-page headline and standfirst that would run if this launched and worked — the way that publication actually writes. A punchy version and a serious version. Then lay the winning one out as one mock front-page slide: masthead, headline, standfirst, captioned image space, division colour with navy ink. Self-check: reads like real journalism not a slogan; no overclaiming; strong hierarchy; one memorable hook; nothing cut off; no AI-slop phrasing.
Social post

Best for a consumer idea that lives or dies in the feed.

STEP 1 — shape (send first): Our idea: [it — rough is fine]. Division: [brand, or "you pick"]. Platform: [Instagram / TikTok / LinkedIn, or "you choose what fits the audience"]. If our idea is vague, sharpen it first and tell us what to. Then propose, don't wait: the one visual hook that would stop the scroll, and one named creator or brand account whose style to echo. Ask us to confirm or swap. STEP 2 — build: Build a single social post mock for that platform, built around the division colour: the scroll-stopping visual described or laid out, the caption in the brand's voice, a clear call to action. Native to the platform, not a corporate ad dropped in. Self-check: would a real person stop on this; voice matches the brand; one scroll-stopping hook; no slop gradient or filler caption.
Name & tagline

Best for any idea that needs an identity to feel real.

Our idea: [it — rough is fine]. Division: [brand, or "you pick"]. If our idea is vague, sharpen it to one concrete thing first and tell us what to. Then give us five name options and a tagline for each — safe to bold. One line each on why it works. Rank them, then ask which direction we lean before refining. Hold this bar: names you could say out loud and put on a pack, not portmanteau word-salad; the tagline says what it does for people, with one memorable turn of phrase — no generic slop.
Concept ad — a 15-second idea

Best for an idea with an emotional or story angle.

Our idea: [it — rough is fine]. Division: [brand, or "you pick"]. If our idea is vague, sharpen it to one concrete thing first and tell us what to. Then write a 15-second ad concept: opening shot, what happens, the line it ends on. Then describe the single key frame in enough detail to picture on screen, and lay it out as one slide in the division's colour with the end line as the headline. Self-check: tells a story in one move, not a feature list; the end line lands; division colour dominant, one strong image idea that carries it; no slop.

The two-minute pitch

For every team, at the end. What you stand up and say to win.

Our canvas — shift: [..]; idea: [..]; assets we made: [list]; how we'd experiment to prove it: [..]. Draft a 2-minute spoken pitch: hook with the real shift we saw, present the idea, show how the assets bring it to life, say how we'd prove it's worth pursuing, close on why ABF should back us over the other teams. Short sentences, open and close land hard. Stay true to our canvas — don't inflate it. Then ask which line feels least true, and help fix it. Optional deck: four slides, one idea each, division colour, minimal text. Self-check against the QUALITY BAR before showing.
If an asset comes out weak — rescue it

When something isn't good enough to win in front of CIOs.

That's not good enough to win in front of CIOs yet. Tell us honestly what's making it look amateur — the idea, the copy, the layout, or the lack of a strong visual? Pick the single biggest problem, tell us what a great version would do differently, and rebuild it to that bar.
